\begin{abstract}
This project investigates customer churn prediction in the telecom industry using 
Logistic Regression and Random Forest. The dataset contains over 500,000 customer 
records with features including support call frequency, payment delay, contract 
length, and total spend. Model performance is evaluated using Accuracy, Sensitivity, 
Specificity, F1-score, and AUC-ROC.

Initial results reveal a significant discrepancy between training accuracy (89.5\%) 
and test accuracy (57.1\%), with a specificity of only 19.4\%. Through distribution analysis, we identify a distributional inconsistency 
between the original train and test sets. Following a random re-split, Logistic 
Regression achieves a test accuracy of 84.8\% with an AUC of 0.908, while Random 
Forest achieves 93.6\% accuracy and an AUC of 0.953. Feature selection using BIC 
removed \textit{Tenure} with no impact on performance, and coefficient analysis 
identified \textit{Contract Length} (Monthly odds ratio = 8.10) and 
\textit{Support Calls} (odds ratio = 1.50) as the strongest churn predictors.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Customer churn refers to the rate at which customers discontinue their subscription 
or cease using a company's products and services within a given period. For telecom 
companies, churn is a critical business metric, as retaining existing customers is 
substantially more cost-effective than acquiring new ones. Identifying customers at 
risk of churning enables companies to intervene proactively, through targeted 
retention strategies, personalized offers, or service improvements, before the 
customer decides to leave.

This project investigates customer churn prediction on a telecom customer dataset 
of over 500,000 records using Logistic Regression and Random Forest. Beyond 
predictive modeling, this work emphasizes identifying and addressing data quality 
issues that affect model reliability. Through exploratory data analysis, we uncover 
a distribution shift in the original train/test split and demonstrate that 
correcting the data partitioning substantially improves generalization performance. 
We further compare feature selection methods (AIC and BIC) and evaluate whether 
non-linear models offer improved predictive accuracy over logistic regression.

\section{Data}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Dataset Overview}
The dataset contains 505,207 customer records sourced from Kaggle, representing 
telecom subscribers over a 12-month observation period. The dataset is pre-divided 
into a training set (440,833 observations) and a test set (64,374 observations). 
During preprocessing, one incomplete observation was removed from the training set, 
resulting in a final training set of 440,832 records. The target variable 
\textit{Churn} is binary, where 1 indicates a customer who discontinued their 
subscription and 0 indicates a retained customer. The training set contains 
190,833 non-churned (43.3\%) and 249,999 churned (56.7\%) customers, indicating 
a mild class imbalance.

\subsection{Feature Description}

The dataset contains 11 variables: 1 identifier (CustomerID, excluded from analysis), 
1 binary target (Churn), and 10 predictors. Features span demographic (Age, Gender), 
behavioral (Usage Frequency, Support Calls, Payment Delay, Total Spend, Last 
Interaction), and contractual (Tenure, Subscription Type, Contract Length) dimensions. 
Among the 10 predictors, 3 are categorical and 7 are continuous.

\subsection{Feature Correlation}

Support Calls shows the strongest positive correlation with Churn ($r = 0.57$), 
consistent with the pattern observed in Section~3.2, confirming that higher support 
call frequency is a strong indicator of churn risk. Total Spend shows the strongest 
negative correlation ($r = -0.43$), suggesting that customers with higher spending 
are less likely to leave. This is intuitive: loyal, high-value customers tend to be 
more engaged with the service, while customers who are already considering leaving 
may have reduced their usage and spending before eventually churning. The remaining 
features show weak to moderate correlations with Churn.

\subsection{Multicollinearity Check (VIF)}

To assess multicollinearity among predictors, Variance Inflation Factors (VIF) 
were computed for all features. VIF measures the extent to which each feature 
can be predicted by the remaining features, with values above 10 generally 
indicating problematic collinearity. All features return VIF values below 1.15, 
well within the acceptable range, confirming that the predictors are largely 
independent of one another and that all features can be retained for modeling.

\section{Methodology}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Feature Selection}
Backward elimination was applied using both criteria, first on the original
training set and subsequently on the re-split training set (see
Section~4.3). AIC penalizes model complexity with a fixed cost per
parameter, while BIC applies a stricter penalty that scales with sample
size, making it more conservative in large datasets. With over 400,000
observations, BIC provides a more rigorous test of whether each variable
contributes meaningfully to prediction.

\subsection{Machine Learning Models}
\paragraph{Logistic Regression}
Logistic Regression was selected as the primary model for its interpretability,
providing coefficient estimates and odds ratios that translate directly into
actionable business insights. Two variants were fitted: a full model using
AIC selection, and a reduced model using BIC selection.

\paragraph{Random Forest}
Random Forest was included as a non-linear benchmark to evaluate whether the
linear assumptions of logistic regression limit predictive performance. Due to
computational constraints, the model was trained on a random subsample of
50,000 observations. All trees use the full set of 10 predictors, as Random
Forest internally handles variable importance through its splitting mechanism.

\subsection{Data Re-partitioning}
Initial evaluation of the logistic regression model revealed a significant 
discrepancy between training and test performance, prompting an investigation 
into data quality. Distribution analysis identified a distributional inconsistency 
between the original train and test sets. To address this, the two sets were 
merged and randomly re-split into a new training set (80\%, 404,164 observations) 
and test set (20\%, 101,042 observations) using a fixed random seed for 
reproducibility. All subsequent models were trained and evaluated on this 
corrected partition.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
Model performance was assessed using Accuracy, Sensitivity, Specificity, 
F1-score, and AUC-ROC. These metrics were chosen to capture different aspects 
of classification performance, particularly given the asymmetric costs of 
misclassifying churners versus non-churners in a business context.

The logistic regression model achieved a training accuracy of
89.5\%, but test accuracy dropped to 57.1\%. Sensitivity was 99.1\% while
Specificity was only 19.4\%, indicating the model predicted nearly all
observations as churned, misclassifying the majority of non-churners.

To investigate, density plots were generated for the two most influential
predictors, Support Calls and Total Spend, stratified by churn label across
train and test sets.

\section{Discussion \& Conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Key Findings}

The logistic regression coefficient analysis identified \textit{Contract Length}, 
\textit{Support Calls}, and \textit{Gender} as the features with the largest 
effect sizes. Monthly contract holders exhibited the highest churn risk with an 
odds ratio of 8.10 relative to Annual customers, each additional 
\textit{Support Call} increased churn odds by 50\%, and male customers showed 
approximately half the churn odds of female customers. The Random Forest variable 
importance ranking highlighted \textit{Support Calls}, \textit{Total Spend}, and 
\textit{Payment Delay} as the top predictors. While the two models rank features 
differently due to their distinct modeling assumptions, \textit{Support Calls} 
consistently emerged as a top predictor across both approaches.

Beyond predictive performance, this analysis uncovered a distributional 
inconsistency between the original train and test sets. This highlights the 
importance of verifying data quality before modeling: applying a pre-defined 
split without examining distributional consistency can lead to misleading 
evaluations, regardless of how well the model itself is specified.

\subsection{Business Recommendations}

\textit{Contract Length} exhibited the largest effect on churn, with Monthly 
subscribers showing 8.10 times the churn odds of Annual customers. Offering 
incentives such as discounts or added benefits to encourage upgrades to 
Quarterly or Annual plans could substantially reduce churn risk. Notably, 
Quarterly and Annual customers showed no significant difference in churn 
behavior ($p = 0.314$), suggesting that any longer-term commitment is 
effective.

\textit{Support Calls} was consistently identified as a top predictor by both 
logistic regression (odds ratio = 1.50) and Random Forest (highest variable 
importance). Each additional support call increased churn odds by 50\%, 
suggesting that high call volumes reflect unresolved customer issues. Improving 
first-contact resolution and proactively addressing recurring complaints may 
help retain at-risk customers.

Female customers exhibited approximately twice the churn odds of male customers, 
suggesting a need for targeted retention efforts for this segment.

\subsection{Limitations}

First, while Random Forest outperformed logistic regression across all metrics, 
confirming that non-linear relationships exist in the data, it comes at the 
cost of interpretability. The logistic regression model remains more suitable 
for deriving actionable business insights through its coefficient estimates 
and odds ratios. Additionally, the Random Forest was trained on a subsample 
of 50,000 observations due to computational constraints, which may not fully 
represent the complete dataset.

Second, the dataset is cross-sectional, providing only a snapshot of customer 
behavior at a single point in time. Without longitudinal data, it is not possible 
to track how individual customer behavior evolves over time or to identify early 
warning signs of churn before they fully manifest.
