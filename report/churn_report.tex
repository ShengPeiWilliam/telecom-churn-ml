\begin{abstract}
This project investigates customer churn prediction in the telecom industry using 
logistic regression. The dataset contains over 500,000 customer records with 
features including support call frequency, payment delay, contract length, and 
total spend. Model performance is evaluated using accuracy, sensitivity, and 
specificity on a held-out test set.

Initial results reveal a significant discrepancy between training accuracy (89.5\%) 
and test accuracy (57.1\%), with a sensitivity of only 19.4\% for the churned class. 
Through distribution analysis, we identify a distributional inconsistency between 
the original train and test sets, particularly within the non-churned group. 
Following a random re-split of the data, the model achieves a test accuracy of 
84.8\% with balanced sensitivity (84.7\%) and specificity (84.8\%), demonstrating 
that the original performance degradation was attributable to data partitioning 
rather than model limitations.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Customer churn refers to the rate at which customers discontinue their subscription 
or cease using a company's products and services within a given period. For telecom 
companies, churn is a critical business metric, as retaining existing customers is 
substantially more cost-effective than acquiring new ones. Identifying customers at 
risk of churning enables companies to intervene proactively, through targeted 
retention strategies, personalized offers, or service improvements, before the 
customer decides to leave.

This project investigates customer churn prediction using Logistic Regression on a 
telecom customer dataset of over 500,000 records. Rather than focusing solely on 
maximizing classifier performance, this work emphasizes identifying and addressing 
data quality issues that affect model reliability. Through exploratory data analysis 
and model evaluation, we uncover a distribution shift in the original train/test 
split, analyze its cause, and demonstrate that correcting the data partitioning 
substantially improves generalization performance.

\section{Data}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Dataset Overview}
The dataset contains 505,207 customer records sourced from Kaggle, representing 
telecom subscribers over a 12-month observation period. The dataset is pre-divided 
into a training set (440,833 observations) and a test set (64,374 observations). 
During preprocessing, one incomplete observation was removed from the training set, 
resulting in a final training set of 440,832 records. The target variable 
\textit{Churn} is binary, where 1 indicates a customer who discontinued their 
subscription and 0 indicates a retained customer. The training set contains 
190,833 non-churned (43.3\%) and 249,999 churned (56.7\%) customers, indicating 
a mild class imbalance.

\subsection{Feature Description}

The dataset contains 11 variables: 1 identifier (CustomerID, excluded from analysis), 
1 binary target (Churn), and 10 predictors. Features span demographic (Age, Gender), 
behavioral (Usage Frequency, Support Calls, Payment Delay, Total Spend, Last 
Interaction), and contractual (Tenure, Subscription Type, Contract Length) dimensions. 
Among the 10 predictors, 3 are categorical and 7 are continuous.

\subsection{Feature Correlation}

Support Calls shows the strongest positive correlation with Churn ($r = 0.57$), 
consistent with the pattern observed in Section~3.2, confirming that higher support 
call frequency is a strong indicator of churn risk. Total Spend shows the strongest 
negative correlation ($r = -0.43$), suggesting that customers with higher spending 
are less likely to leave. This is intuitive: loyal, high-value customers tend to be 
more engaged with the service, while customers who are already considering leaving 
may have reduced their usage and spending before eventually churning. The remaining 
features show weak to moderate correlations with Churn.

\subsection{Multicollinearity Check (VIF)}

To assess multicollinearity among predictors, Variance Inflation Factors (VIF) 
were computed for all features. VIF measures the extent to which each feature 
can be predicted by the remaining features, with values above 10 generally 
indicating problematic collinearity. All features return VIF values below 1.15, 
well within the acceptable range, confirming that the predictors are largely 
independent of one another and that all features can be retained for modeling

\section{Methodology}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Feature Selection (Backward Elimination)}

Backward elimination was applied using AIC as the selection criterion. Starting 
from the full model, features are iteratively removed if their exclusion reduces 
the AIC. All features were retained after a single iteration, indicating that 
each predictor contributes meaningful information to the model.

Notably, Contract Length shows an asymmetric pattern: the Monthly coefficient 
is large and highly significant ($p < 0.001$), while the Quarterly coefficient 
is not significant ($p = 0.42$), suggesting that Quarterly and Annual customers 
exhibit similar churn behavior, whereas Monthly subscribers are substantially 
more likely to churn.

The model coefficients are stable across all features, with small standard errors 
relative to their estimates, indicating reliable parameter estimation given the 
large sample size.

\subsection{Logistic Regression}

The model achieved a mean cross-validated 
accuracy of 89.5\% and a Kappa of 0.788, indicating strong agreement between 
predicted and actual churn labels beyond chance.

\section{Results}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Logistic Regression (Original Split)}

The model was evaluated on the held-out test set of 64,374 observations. Despite 
achieving a cross-validated training accuracy of 89.5\%, test accuracy dropped 
significantly to 57.1\% (Kappa = 0.177), suggesting a substantial generalization 
problem. Sensitivity for the non-churned class was only 19.4\%, while Specificity 
reached 99.1\%, indicating that the model correctly identified nearly all churned 
customers but failed to recognize the majority of non-churned customers, 
classifying them as churned instead.

This discrepancy between training and test performance initially suggested 
overfitting. To investigate further, we examined the feature distributions of 
the most influential predictors identified in the correlation analysis, 
specifically Support Calls ($r = 0.57$) and Total Spend ($r = -0.43$).

\subsection{Distribution Shift Analysis}

To investigate the root cause of the performance degradation, we examined the 
feature distributions across the original train and test splits. Density plots 
were generated for the two most influential predictors---\texttt{Support.Calls} 
and \texttt{Total.Spend}---stratified by churn label.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/dist_support_calls.png}
    \caption{Density plot of \texttt{Support.Calls} by churn label across train and test sets.}
    \label{fig:dist_support}
\end{figure}

As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:dist_support}, the \texttt{Support.Calls} distribution 
for the \textit{Not Churn} group in the training set is concentrated at lower 
values, clearly separated from the \textit{Churn} group. However, the 
\textit{Not Churn} group in the test set follows a markedly different distribution, 
overlapping substantially with the training set's \textit{Churn} group. In other 
words, many non-churners in the test set exhibit call volumes typically associated 
with churners in the training set.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/dist_total_spend.png}
    \caption{Density plot of \texttt{Total.Spend} by churn label across train and test sets.}
    \label{fig:dist_spend}
\end{figure}
A similar pattern is observed in Figure~\ref{fig:dist_spend}, where test-set 
non-churners exhibit a \texttt{Total.Spend} distribution that more closely 
resembles training-set churners than their own training-set counterparts.

Taken together, these distributional inconsistencies suggest that the original 
train and test sets were not drawn from the same underlying population. The 
model's poor test performance therefore reflects a data quality issue rather 
than a deficiency in the modeling approach.

\subsection{Logistic Regression (Re-split)}

To address the distribution shift identified in the previous section, the original 
train and test sets were merged and randomly re-split into 80\% training and 20\% 
testing using a fixed random seed. The same preprocessing pipeline and feature 
set were applied to the new split.

The re-trained model achieved a cross-validated training accuracy of 84.9\% 
(Kappa = 0.695) and a test accuracy of 84.8\% (Kappa = 0.692), with a negligible 
gap of 0.1 percentage points between the two. Table~\ref{tab:results_comparison} 
summarizes the performance metrics across both splits.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \caption{Model Performance Comparison: Original Split vs.\ Re-split}
    \label{tab:results_comparison}
    \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
        \toprule
        & \textbf{CV Acc.} & \textbf{Test Acc.} & \textbf{Kappa} & \textbf{Sensitivity} & \textbf{Specificity} \\
        \midrule
        Original Split & 89.5\% & 57.1\% & 0.177 & 0.194 & 0.991 \\
        Re-split       & 84.9\% & 84.8\% & 0.692 & 0.847 & 0.848 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Notably, the original split yielded a sensitivity of only 19.4\%, indicating that 
the model failed to identify the vast majority of actual churners in the test set. 
This extreme imbalance between sensitivity and specificity further reflects the 
distributional mismatch: faced with test-set churners whose feature profiles 
resembled training-set non-churners, the model defaulted to predicting 
\textit{Not Churn} for most observations. After re-splitting, sensitivity and 
specificity converge to 84.7\% and 84.8\% respectively, demonstrating a balanced and consistent model under consistent data conditions.

This alignment confirms that the original performance degradation was driven by 
the distributional inconsistency in the data split rather than the model itself.

\section{Discussion \& Conclusion}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Key Findings}

The analysis identified \texttt{Support.Calls}, \texttt{Total.Spend}, and 
\texttt{Payment.Delay} as the strongest predictors of customer churn, consistent 
with their high correlations observed during exploratory data analysis.

Beyond predictive performance, this analysis also uncovered a distributional 
inconsistency between the original train and test sets. This highlights the 
importance of verifying data quality before modeling: applying a pre-defined 
split without examining distributional consistency can lead to misleading 
evaluations, regardless of how well the model itself is specified.

\subsection{Business Recommendations}

Since \texttt{Support.Calls} is the strongest predictor of churn, reducing 
customer frustration should be a priority. High call volumes likely indicate 
unresolved issues. Improving first-contact resolution and addressing recurring 
complaints early may help retain at-risk customers.

Monthly subscribers are also significantly more likely to churn than those on 
longer contracts. Offering discounts or added benefits to encourage upgrades to 
quarterly or annual plans could improve retention by increasing customer commitment.

\subsection{Limitations}

This analysis is subject to several limitations. First, logistic regression assumes 
a linear relationship between each predictor and the log-odds of churn. If the 
true relationships are non-linear, the model may fail to capture more complex 
patterns in the data.

Second, the dataset is cross-sectional, providing only a snapshot of customer 
behavior at a single point in time. Without longitudinal data, it is not possible 
to track how individual customer behavior evolves over time or to identify early 
warning signs of churn before they fully manifest.
